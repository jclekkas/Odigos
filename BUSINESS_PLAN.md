# Odigos — Lite Business Plan

## What It Is

**Odigos** is a consumer protection tool for U.S. car buyers. Users paste a dealer quote, email, or text message. Odigos analyzes it in seconds and returns a GO/NO-GO verdict with a list of detected issues — hidden fees, missing out-the-door pricing, forced add-ons, and common dealership negotiation traps.

The product is split into two tiers:

- **Free tier:** The GO/NO-GO verdict and surface-level analysis
- **Paid tier ($49, one-time):** Full deal review — missing information checklist, suggested reply script, detailed reasoning, and confidence assessment

No account required. No subscription. No friction.

---

## The Problem

Buying a car is one of the largest financial transactions most Americans will ever make — and dealers operate with a significant information advantage. The monthly payment trap, dealer-installed add-ons, vague "market adjustments," and incomplete quotes are structural features of the dealership industry, not edge cases.

The out-the-door price — the actual total cost to drive home — is routinely obscured. A buyer who agrees to a "great deal" at $42,000 may leave the dealership having paid $47,600 once fees, add-ons, and financing adjustments are applied.

Most buyers know something feels wrong. They don't know what it is or how to respond.

---

## The Solution

Odigos reads the quote the buyer already has and tells them exactly what to look for. It doesn't require a VIN, a dealer name, or any account setup. It works on any text the buyer can copy and paste — dealer emails, texts, screenshots, PDFs.

The output is structured as a consumer-protection document: a clear verdict, a list of red flags with explanations, and a ready-to-send response the buyer can use to request corrections.

## Market Size

**Total Addressable Market (TAM)**
Approximately 14 million new cars and 36 million used cars are sold annually in the U.S. — roughly 50 million vehicle transactions per year. Every one of those buyers receives some form of dealer communication.

**Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)**
Buyers who research purchases online and are price-aware — broadly estimated at 40–60% of all buyers. That's 20–30 million people per year who are actively looking for information and validation.

**Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM) — near-term**
Even 0.1% conversion across a 20 million buyer SAM at $49/transaction = ~$10M/year in revenue. With strong SEO as the distribution engine, this is an achievable medium-term target with essentially no sales overhead.

## Pricing & Economics

- **$49 one-time** per analysis unlock
- No subscription, no account, no support overhead
- OpenAI API cost per analysis: approximately $0.02–0.10 depending on quote length
- Stripe processing: ~3%
- Gross margin per transaction at $49: **~95%**
- No fulfillment, no inventory, no CAC from paid channels if SEO is working

The one-time pricing is intentional. It matches the transaction model — a buyer buys one car, reviews one quote. Subscription pricing creates friction and misaligns with the customer's natural behavior.

## Distribution

**Primary: SEO content**
The article pages target high-intent, long-tail search terms where buyers are actively looking for help — "dealer doc fee by state," "are dealer add-ons mandatory," "how to calculate out-the-door price," "dealer added fees after agreement." These queries are mid-to-bottom funnel: the person searching has a problem right now. Each article ends with a direct CTA to the analyzer.

**Secondary: Organic/word-of-mouth**
Car buying is a social experience. Buyers share research, deals, and horror stories. A tool that gives someone a clear answer to "is this deal good?" is naturally shareable.

**Tertiary: Community distribution**
Reddit communities (r/askcarsales, r/personalfinance, r/frugal), Facebook groups, and consumer advocacy forums are high-density audiences with exactly the right problem. Not a paid channel — an organic one.

## Competitive Landscape

| Product | Model | What They Do | Key Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TrueCar / Edmunds / KBB** | Ad revenue / lead gen | Pricing databases | No analysis of an actual quote the buyer has |
| **CarEdge** | $99–$399/month coaching | Human coaching + data | Subscription, high friction, not instant |
| **Dealer prep** | Free | Dealer-controlled info | Conflict of interest |
| **r/askcarsales** | Free | Community advice | Not scalable, asynchronous |
| **Odigos** | $49 one-time | Instant AI analysis of the buyer's specific quote | First-mover in this specific format |

The most direct competitor isn't a product — it's the Reddit thread. Buyers paste their deal to r/askcarsales and wait hours for strangers to answer. Odigos does the same thing in 10 seconds for $49.

No existing consumer product sits in the exact position Odigos occupies: **frictionless, specific, instant, and priced per transaction.**

## What Odigos Is Not

- Not a pricing database (no VIN lookup, no MSRP comparison)
- Not a coaching service (no humans, no scheduling)
- Not a subscription product
- Not affiliated with any dealership, OEM, or financing company

This independence is a core part of the value proposition. Odigos has no incentive to steer a buyer toward any particular dealer or outcome.

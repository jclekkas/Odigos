How to Ask a Dealer for an Out-the-Door Price
When you’re shopping for a car, the most important number isn’t the sticker price. It’s the out-the-door price.
The out-the-door price (OTD) is the total amount you will actually pay to drive the car off the lot. It includes the vehicle price, taxes, registration, dealer fees, and any add-ons.
If a dealer only quotes the vehicle price or a monthly payment, you still don’t know what the car really costs.
Before visiting a dealership, you should always ask for the full out-the-door price in writing.
Why You Should Ask for the OTD Price First
Many buyers walk into a dealership with only the vehicle price or payment quote.
The problem is that several charges often get added later, including:
• Sales tax
• Title and registration
• Dealer documentation fees
• Dealer add-ons
• Market adjustment fees
These can add thousands of dollars to the final price.
When you ask for the full OTD price before visiting, you remove most of the uncertainty.
You also make it easier to compare offers between dealerships.
The Best Way to Ask for the Price
The easiest way to get a clear answer is to ask by text or email.
Written quotes prevent misunderstandings and make it harder for numbers to change later.
You can use a simple message like this:
Example message
“Can you send me the full out-the-door price in writing, including vehicle price, dealer fees, taxes, registration, and any add-ons?”
This tells the dealer exactly what you expect in the quote.
What the Out-the-Door Price Should Include
A proper OTD price breakdown usually includes:
Vehicle sale price
The negotiated price of the car itself.
Sales tax
State and sometimes local tax based on where the car will be registered.
Title and registration fees
Fees required by your state to register the vehicle.
Dealer documentation fee
A fee charged by the dealership for paperwork.
Any dealer add-ons
Items like paint protection, VIN etching, wheel locks, or nitrogen-filled tires.
Some of these charges are normal. Others may be optional or negotiable.
If a dealer refuses to include them in the quote, the number you received is not the real price.
What to Do If the Dealer Avoids the Question
Sometimes dealers will respond with something like:
• “Come into the dealership and we’ll go over it.”
• “We can discuss the final numbers in person.”
• “Let’s talk about monthly payments instead.”
These responses usually mean the dealership prefers not to provide the full number yet.
You can reply politely but directly:
“I’m comparing offers from several dealers and just need the full out-the-door price in writing before scheduling a visit.”
Most dealers will provide it when asked clearly.
If they refuse, you may want to consider another dealership.
You can also read what to do if a dealer won’t give an out-the-door price before you visit.
Red Flags to Watch For
A few common warning signs appear when buyers ask for OTD pricing.
Incomplete price breakdown
If the quote only shows the vehicle price, taxes may still be missing.
Add-ons listed without prices
Sometimes dealers list products but don’t include the cost until later.
Sudden new charges
If the OTD price changes significantly when you arrive, ask for a full explanation.
Before You Go to the Dealership
Once you receive the quote, review it carefully.
Make sure the breakdown includes:
• Vehicle price
• Taxes
• Registration
• Dealer fees
• Add-ons
If anything seems unclear, ask for clarification before visiting.

FAQ
Do dealers have to give an out-the-door price?
No law requires dealers to provide one immediately, but many will when asked directly.
Should I negotiate using MSRP or OTD price?
The OTD price is usually the best number to compare because it reflects the real total cost.
Can the OTD price change later?
It can change if new items are added or if the quote was incomplete. That’s why getting the breakdown in writing is important.

OTD Price vs. MSRP: What Car Buyers Get Wrong
Many car buyers focus on the MSRP when comparing vehicles.
But the MSRP is not the final price you pay.
The number that matters most is the out-the-door price, which includes taxes, fees, and other charges.
Understanding the difference can help you avoid surprises at the dealership.
What MSRP Actually Means
MSRP stands for Manufacturer’s Suggested Retail Price.
It is the price recommended by the car manufacturer for the vehicle.
The MSRP usually includes:
• Base vehicle price
• Factory-installed options
• Destination charge
However, it does not include taxes, registration, or most dealer fees.
That means the amount you actually pay will usually be higher.
What the Out-the-Door Price Includes
The out-the-door price represents the total cost to purchase the car.
It typically includes:
• Vehicle sale price
• Sales tax
• Title and registration
• Dealer documentation fee
• Dealer add-ons or accessories
Because it includes everything, the OTD price is the most accurate number for comparing offers between dealerships.
Why Monthly Payment Quotes Confuse Buyers
Many dealerships focus on the monthly payment instead of the total price.
Monthly payments depend on several variables:
• loan term
• interest rate
• down payment
• trade-in value
Two offers with the same payment may have very different total costs.
That’s why it’s usually better to compare out-the-door price first, then discuss financing separately.
What Gets Added on Top of MSRP
Several charges often appear after the vehicle price is discussed.
These may include:
Sales tax
Based on your state’s tax rate.
Title and registration fees
Required to legally register the car.
Dealer documentation fee
A fee for processing paperwork.
Dealer add-ons
Optional products installed by the dealership.
These additions explain why the final price often differs significantly from the MSRP.
Which Number Should You Compare?
When comparing dealerships, focus on the out-the-door price.
The OTD price shows the full amount you will pay.
Two dealers may offer the same vehicle at the same MSRP but have very different OTD totals because of fees or add-ons.
Before visiting the dealership, ask for the full out-the-door price in writing.
If a dealer avoids providing it, that can be a sign the final cost may be higher than expected.
Example
A car with a $30,000 MSRP might end up with an out-the-door price closer to:
• $31,800 after tax
• $32,200 after registration and doc fees
• $33,000 or more if add-ons are included
This is why understanding the OTD price is essential when negotiating.
Before Visiting the Dealership
Once you receive a written quote, review the breakdown carefully.
Make sure the OTD price includes every major charge.
